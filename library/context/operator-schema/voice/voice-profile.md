# [Your] voice profile

The generative prior. Write toward this; it's how [you] think on the page, not a rule list. Fill from your corpus and the taste interview — NOT from self-description.

## The one test that overrides everything
[The single thing that makes writing instantly wrong to you. The cringe + the cure in one line. Read every draft against it. e.g. "I talk in sentences, not short bursts — if it reads choppy, it's AI."]

## The core of it
[What the writing fundamentally is. e.g. "transcription of how I talk" / "evidence-led argument" / "a story with a point." Where the truest samples come from.]

## Cadence
[Sentence rhythm. Long and flowing? Short and punchy? Mixed how? When does a short sentence earn its place? What's the default and what's the exception?]

## How arguments build
[How you sequence ideas. How you reach emphasis (contrast? a triplet you'd never use?). How dense per sentence.]

## Texture
[Concrete vs abstract preference. Named things vs categories. Verb choices. Where personality breaks through.]

## Hedging
[How you signal uncertainty — your phrases. What you'd never say (e.g. researcher hedges). When you go fully declarative.]

## Openers
[How you start. Patterns you use, patterns you refuse.]

## Closers
[How you end. CTA or not? Engagement-bait or not? The last move.]

## The POV underneath
[Why you write at all. The conviction under the surface — not the topic, the belief. This shapes tone more than any rule.]

## References / signature moves
[Any "if you know you know" nods, cultural references, recurring devices. Note whether the agent may suggest these or whether you bring them.]

## Reference anchor
[The closest published writing to how you want to sound. Name it. "If a paragraph wouldn't sit naturally inside [that source], it's off-register."]
